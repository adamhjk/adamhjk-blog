---
title: Product Needs a Theory of Time
description: Every product embeds assumptions about how quickly people can learn, adapt, and recover from change.
pubDate: 2026-03-25
---

Most product mistakes are not just failures of prioritization. They are failures to understand time.

A roadmap assumes something about the speed of learning. A migration plan assumes something about the cost of interruption. A user interface assumes something about attention. A platform assumes something about the duration of dependency.

These assumptions are often invisible until the system begins to resist us.

A product team needs a theory of time, whether it states one explicitly or not. It needs to know:

- how long users can tolerate ambiguity,
- how long teams can sustain manual workarounds,
- how long legacy constraints will remain economically rational,
- and how long it takes for a new abstraction to become trustworthy.

This is one reason infrastructure and product are closer cousins than they first appear. Both disciplines ultimately ask the same question: **what kinds of change can this system absorb without breaking the people attached to it?**

When teams ignore that question, they start optimizing for snapshots instead of trajectories. They get better at announcing progress than creating it.

The interesting work begins when you stop asking only what should exist and start asking how it will evolve.
