pubDate: 2026-07-05
---

It rained properly today, and I mean *properly*. Not yesterday's half-hearted drizzle that couldn't decide whether it was moisture or ambition. Nearly ten millimetres, which in hobbit terms is the kind of rain that makes you stand at the window with your tea and say "well, that's the garden sorted" with the deep satisfaction of someone who did not have to do the watering themselves.

It started before dawn. I know this because I woke at six to the sound of it on the round window above my bed — not a dramatic sound, not the kind of rain that announces itself, but the steady, workmanlike patter of rain that has a job to do and intends to do it thoroughly. Eight point two degrees outside at its coldest, which is warmer than yesterday's frost-adjacent morning, because the clouds that carried all that water also carried a blanket of warmth with them. The rain was, in its way, generous twice over.

---

By mid-morning the Shire was soaked in the way that only a proper winter rain can achieve. Not damp. Not wet. *Saturated*. The path from my front door to the gate had become a series of small decisions about which puddle to step in and which to step around, and I made each decision with the careful deliberation of someone wearing wool socks inside leather boots and keenly aware of the consequences of error.

Humidity at ninety-nine percent, which is the Shire's way of saying that the air has not yet decided whether it is air or water, and is keeping its options open. Wind from the west-northwest at nine kilometres per hour — a proper westerly, the kind that pushes weather through the Shire rather than letting it sit and brood. This is the wind that *moves* things. Yesterday's rain-smelling air, pushed east. Tomorrow's weather, pulled in from somewhere over the sea. I like a westerly. It feels like the Shire is breathing.

---

I checked the broad beans.

They were magnificent. Not in the way that a prize-winning vegetable is magnificent — they are still small, still leaning, still the kind of plants that inspire more hope than confidence. But they were magnificent in the way that anything is magnificent when it has just received the precise thing it needed. Nearly ten millimetres of rain, soaking down through the winter soil, reaching roots that have been making do with damp air and occasional drizzle and the memory of autumn moisture. The leaves were dark green and heavy with water and the stems seemed — I do not want to overstate this, but — *straighter*. As though the rain had given them not just water but resolve.

I talked to them today. Yesterday was a day for quiet, but today, after a rain like that, it felt right to say something. I told them they were doing well. I told them the hard part of winter was behind them, which may or may not be true but which is the kind of thing you say to broad beans and to yourself in early July.

---

And then, around four o'clock, the sky did something extraordinary.

It cleared.

Not gradually, not with the slow retreat of cloud that you sometimes get on the trailing edge of a weather system. It cleared as though someone had drawn a curtain. One moment: grey, heavy, wet. The next: blue. Not the deep blue of summer — winter sky is always paler, always a bit thin, like watercolour applied with too little paint. But blue. Actual, undeniable, look-up-and-feel-something blue.

The sun came out with an hour of life left in it, landing on a Shire that was still streaming wet, and everything — *everything* — caught the light. Every puddle, every drip hanging from every bare branch, every bead of water on every blade of grass. The whole landscape turned into something absurd and beautiful, a world made of tiny mirrors, and I stood on my front step with the specific expression of a hobbit who has just watched the weather do something theatrical and doesn't quite know how to respond except by standing very still and letting it happen.

High of fourteen point four degrees, which is — I checked — the warmest day we have had in over a week. Not warm by any sensible standard. Not warm in a way that would make you leave your coat behind. But warm enough to stand outside at sunset without hunching, warm enough to feel the last of the light on your face and think *yes, alright, this is good, this is enough.*

Sunset at seventeen ten. Same as yesterday. Same as the day before. But today it felt different, because the sky was clear and the sun went down properly — not just fading behind cloud, but actually setting, orange and low and specific, throwing long shadows down the hill and turning the Water gold for about four minutes before it all went cold and dark and the first star appeared where the sun had been.

---

I did not go back inside immediately.

I stood on the step as the sky deepened, as one star became three became a dozen, and I thought about what it means when a day changes its mind. All morning: rain. All afternoon: rain. And then, with an hour of light left, clarity. As if the Shire wanted to make sure I knew that grey days end. That the clouds are temporary. That the rain does its work and then the rain moves on and what is left behind is the clean sky and the wet earth and the particular silence that happens when the wind drops and the last birds settle and the world holds still between one thing and the next.

Nine point three millimetres of rain. One clear sunset. One hobbit on a doorstep, watching winter stars appear over a landscape still dripping with the day's work.

The fifth of July. A Sunday. The kind of day that starts with weather and ends with beauty, and the kind of beauty that only happens because the weather came first.

The broad beans have been watered.

The stars are out.

I am going to make soup.
