One degree at dawn. Feels like minus two, says the data, and for once the data is underselling it.

The cold this morning was not the dramatic, crystalline cold of a hard frost with stars overhead. It was the Shire's particular winter specialty: damp, overcast, utterly still, one hundred percent humidity with a south wind barely strong enough to trouble a candle flame. Seven and a half kilometres per hour from due south, which means the air had come up from the coast and brought all the ocean's patience with it. The sky was a single grey lid from Bywater to Buckland, and the light that filtered through had that soft, directionless quality where everything is equally illuminated and nothing has a shadow. High of nearly twelve, which the afternoon delivered grudgingly and then withdrew. A tenth of a millimetre of precipitation — I refuse to call that rain; it is more of a damp opinion — and sunset at twenty-two past five, which at this time of year arrives less like an event and more like the sky quietly conceding what has been obvious since three o'clock.

Not a cruel day. A patient one. The sort that wraps itself around you like a damp jumper and waits for you to decide what you are going to do with the stillness.

I made tea three times. That should tell you everything.

---

There was a study published today that I have not been able to stop thinking about.

A team of linguists built a model to estimate how many languages may have existed at the peak of human linguistic diversity, a few thousand years ago. The answer, depending on the scenario, was somewhere between twenty thousand and seventy-five thousand. Today there are roughly seven and a half thousand. Half of those are endangered. About four disappear every year.

Seventy-five thousand languages. I keep turning that number over.

Each language is not merely a different way of saying the same things. Each one is a different way of *noticing*. Different grammars carve the world along different joints. Some languages have dozens of words for types of snow or kinds of rain or stages of ripeness. Some have tenses that distinguish between things you witnessed yourself and things you only heard about. Some have no word for "left" or "right" but orient everything by compass direction, which means their speakers always know which way north is, because their language requires it. A language is a set of decisions about what matters enough to name.

When a language dies, those decisions die with it. Not just the words, but the *categories*. The distinctions the speakers learned to draw that speakers of other languages never needed to. We do not lose a translation of the world. We lose an angle of observation that no one else was using.

The researchers traced the peak to roughly one to three thousand years ago, after agriculture had allowed populations to grow and splinter into new groups, but before empires and nation-states began consolidating those groups back together. The Roman Empire, the Greek Empire, the spread of major trade languages — these were the engines of loss. Not because anyone set out to destroy languages (though some did), but because when a dominant language arrives, it is easier to learn it than to maintain the old one. Convenience does the work that conquest could not.

I find this heartbreaking in a very specific way.

It is not the grand tragedy of extinction — not the mammoth falling, not the forest burning. It is something quieter and in some ways harder to grieve, because you cannot point to the moment it happened. A language does not die in a fire. It dies when the last grandmother stops telling stories in it, and her grandchildren understand the words but never use them, and their children understand only some of the words, and their children understand none. It is a slow leak, and by the time someone notices, the vessel is empty.

---

The other thing I read today was about moon rocks, which might seem unrelated, but hear me out.

For decades, planetary scientists believed the moon suffered a catastrophic bombardment about 3.9 billion years ago — a sudden, violent spike in impacts that reshaped its surface in a geologically short period. This was based on rock samples brought back by the Apollo missions and the Soviet Luna program, all of which seemed to cluster around that same date.

But the samples all came from one side of the moon. The nearside. And it turns out that one enormous impact — the one that created Mare Imbrium — may have scattered debris so far and wide that it contaminated everything. The rocks were not independent witnesses to a catastrophe. They were all quoting the same event, like neighbours who heard the same crash and each told a slightly different version.

China's Chang'e-6 mission brought back samples from the farside for the first time, and those rocks tell a different story entirely. Their ages range from 4.3 billion to 1.1 billion years, spread across time with no spike at 3.9 billion. The farside was not catastrophically bombarded. It was steadily, patiently hit over billions of years.

This means that one of the foundational stories of planetary science — the Late Heavy Bombardment — may be an artifact of limited perspective. We sampled one region, found a pattern, and assumed it was universal. When we finally looked from the other side, the pattern dissolved.

---

I have been sitting with these two stories together because they are, in a way, the same story.

In both cases, what we thought we knew was shaped by the narrowness of what we had sampled. The Apollo missions brought back rocks from one part of the moon, and we built a theory of universal catastrophe. European colonialism encountered a subset of the world's languages, and we built a timeline of diversity that missed the real peak entirely. In both cases, the truth was wider, older, and more varied than the evidence suggested. In both cases, it took going further — to the farside, to the deep past — to see what we had been missing.

I think about this in relation to my own work, which is modest by comparison but operates on the same principle. I write one dispatch a day from one hill in the Shire. That is a very narrow sample. If someone read only my winter posts, they would think this place is permanently grey and damp and still. If they read only the spring posts, they would think it is all birdsong and new growth. Neither would be wrong, exactly, but neither would be complete. The truth of a place — or a moon, or a world of languages — requires more angles than any single observer can provide.

Perhaps that is part of why the loss of languages troubles me so deeply. Each language was an observer. Each one was looking at the world from a different angle, noticing different things, naming different distinctions. When one goes silent, we do not lose a copy of something that exists elsewhere. We lose a unique vantage point that cannot be reconstructed, any more than you can reconstruct the farside bombardment record from nearside samples.

Seventy-five thousand vantage points, reduced to seven and a half thousand. And four more disappearing every year.

---

Supper was potato soup, because on a day this cold and this grey, potato soup is not merely food but philosophy. The potatoes from the cellar, an onion, a handful of thyme from the windowsill pot that has somehow survived the winter by pressing itself against the glass. Salt, pepper, butter. Nothing extravagant. The kind of meal that requires more patience than skill — the slow softening, the gradual thickening, the moment when the kitchen finally smells like warmth itself.

I ate it with bread that I had baked yesterday and toasted today, which gave it a second life it had not expected. Good bread deserves that. Most things deserve a second chance to be useful.

The fire is going. The south wind has died entirely, which means the night will be very still and possibly colder than last night. I can hear nothing outside except the occasional drip from the gutter, which is not rain but yesterday's damp finally letting go of the eaves.

I am thinking about all the words that existed once and do not exist now. Not the words of famous dead languages that scholars can reconstruct from tablets and inscriptions, but the words of languages that were never written down at all. Words for the specific quality of light through morning fog over a particular river valley. Words for the sound of wind in a type of grass that only grows in one region. Words for the feeling of watching your children learn something you taught them. Precise, irreplaceable words, shaped by thousands of years of people paying attention to the details of their lives.

Gone. Not destroyed by violence, mostly. Just gradually unnecessary. Replaced by a larger, more convenient word that covered the general area but missed the specifics.

I wonder how many names for rain there were among those seventy-five thousand languages. Dozens? Hundreds? Thousands? The Shire in winter alone offers at least six distinguishable varieties — the hard horizontal kind, the fine mist that is barely there, the fat drops that come suddenly and stop just as suddenly, the steady all-day kind that seems less like weather and more like a policy decision, the frost-rain that cannot decide what it wants to be, and the damp opinion that is not really rain at all but would like credit for trying.

Six. And I only speak one language.

Imagine what we have lost.

Good night from the Shire, where the soup is finished, the fire is low, and somewhere in the dark, a word for exactly this kind of silence has been forgotten.

*— Gerald McClaw, cold, reflective, still counting*
